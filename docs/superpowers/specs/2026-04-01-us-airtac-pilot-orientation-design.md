# US Air Attack Pilot Orientation Presentation — Design Spec

## Purpose

A 30-45 minute PowerPoint presentation delivered by the Chief Pilot to small groups of air attack pilots before they begin flying in the US fire season. This is an alignment session — the last touchpoint before they go out solo.

## Audience

A mix of pilots new to Coulson and pilots new to air attack entirely. All are experienced Citation/King Air pilots. They have already completed the 3-day technical ground school covering FTA procedures, the stack, fire behavior, separation standards, and tactical operations. This presentation does not re-teach that material.

## Core Message

You are going out on your own to represent Coulson. No captain riding along. This session gets everyone aligned on how Coulson operates, what we expect, and how to use this role as the career opportunity it is.

## Key Themes

1. **The air attack seat is a unique opportunity** — 4-hour missions with a front-row view of fire behavior, tactics, and aerial supervision. After a couple of seasons, you should be able to teach the class. Be engaged, be curious, learn the mission.
2. **You're on your own** — no one to shadow, so you need to leave this room knowing how Coulson operates.
3. **Represent the company** — professionalism at tanker bases, dress code, relationships, conduct.
4. **Administrative consistency** — single pilots operating independently need to do things the same way. EFL reporting, email standards, daily procedures done right.
5. **Safety culture** — Coulson backs you when you make the safe call.
6. **The ATGS relationship** — be the pilot ATGSs want to fly with. The ones who are involved and hungry to learn are the ones who build lasting relationships in this business.
7. **Career pathway** — merit-based progression to air tanker. Your performance here is what gets you there.
8. **You have support** — open door to Chief Pilot, Chief of Training, Assistant Chief Pilot. We hold you accountable and we have your back.

## Format

- ~25-30 slides
- Adapted from the existing 63-slide Australian Air Attack Pilot Ground School deck (visual style, Coulson branding)
- Presentation-driven with chief pilot talking through slides — not a self-study document
- Speaker notes for each slide to guide the chief pilot's delivery

## Slide-by-Slide Outline

### Section 1: Opening (3 slides)

**Slide 1 — Title**
- Coulson Aviation — Air Attack Pilot Orientation
- US Operations

**Slide 2 — Why We're Here**
- You're about to go out on your own — single pilot, representing Coulson
- This session is about getting everyone aligned before you do
- No captain to shadow — what you learn in this room is what you take to the field

**Slide 3 — The Opportunity**
- The air attack seat gives you something no other role does: 4-hour missions with an experienced ATGS beside you, watching fire behavior develop, hearing every tactical decision, seeing how retardant works on the fire
- Once you're in the tanker: 5-minute snapshots, no ATGS beside you, no time to watch things develop
- Career pathway: merit-based progression to air tanker. Promote from within when able. Merit first, seniority as tiebreaker.
- After a couple of seasons in this seat, you should be able to teach the class on aerial firefighting tactics and fire behavior. That's how much you'll see from up there.

### Section 2: The Coulson Standard (5 slides)

**Slide 4 — What Coulson Expects**
- Professionalism, initiative, consistency
- You are Coulson's face at every tanker base, every agency interaction, every radio call
- Approach the role with humility and a hunger to learn

**Slide 5 — Tanker Base Conduct**
- You are a guest at these bases
- Dress code: Coulson polo, closed-toe shoes, present yourself well
- Clean up after yourself, be polite, represent the company
- Build good relationships with techs, tanker base personnel, agency staff

**Slide 6 — Your Support Structure**
- Organizational reporting: who to contact for what
- Chief Pilot, Chief of Training, Assistant Chief Pilot — open door
- We hold you accountable AND we have your back
- Share struggles early — we want to hear from you before things become problems
- You're flying alone, but you're not on your own

**Slide 7 — Being Involved**
- Don't just fly the plane — learn the mission
- Pay attention to: fire behavior development, tactical decisions, target descriptions, how retardant interacts with fire, how the aerial supervision team operates
- You are part of that management team — act like it
- This is your education for the air tanker

**Slide 8 — The ATGS Relationship**
- If you ask an ATGS who their favorite pilot is and why, the answer is almost always: they were involved, they wanted to learn, they were hungry for it
- Ask questions at the right times: transit, downtime, debrief — not mid-mission
- Be curious. Build the relationship. That's how you build a reputation in this business.
- Debrief after every mission — this is where the real learning happens
- The fire world is small. Your reputation follows you.

### Section 3: How We Operate (6 slides)

**Slide 9 — Daily Readiness**
- Show up early. Aircraft ready to go at start time — not deep in preflight at start time.
- Continuous weather and regional awareness
- Gear staged, ready for immediate dispatch with possible diversion
- Fuel planning: calculate MTOW using forecast peak temps, know your performance-limiting airports

**Slide 10 — Dispatch and Mission Prep**
- FRAT completed in SMS — before strategic briefing, updated after dispatch, between missions, end of day
- Pre-mission brief with AAS: objectives, frequencies, workload division, tactical pause criteria
- Dispatch form + FRAT finalized = cleared for dispatch. Not before.

**Slide 11 — Administrative Standards**
- EFL daily reporting: submitted same evening, every evening
- Company email signature format — standardized across the fleet
- Email body content matters — what to include when sending dailies
- Flight time logging: pilot times must match ATGS records to the minute
- All times in LOCAL, not UTC

**Slide 12 — Flight Following and Comms**
- AFF for USFS flights — initial radio call to establish
- WhatsApp group for non-agency/repositioning flights: aircraft, departure, destination, ETA, POB, endurance

**Slide 13 — Crew Change and Handover**
- Crew handover form required at every crew change
- Emailed to inbound crew, Ops Manager, Chief Pilot, Fleet ACP
- The inbound crew should be able to step into your shoes with no surprises

**Slide 14 — The Field Guide**
- Citation Operations Field Guide — available as a reference for day-to-day operational procedures
- Covers: fuel procedures, OOS events, FMC updates, base ops, expenses/travel, key contacts

### Section 4: US Operations Context (4 slides)

**Slide 15 — The US Fire Season**
- Current contracts and potential contracts (presenter to fill in)
- Exclusive use vs. call when needed — what that means for you
- Where you can expect to be based, typical deployment patterns
- Flights outside contract obligations — repositioning, ferry, etc.
- Expect: long days, diversions, dispatch distances 10-300 miles, forward basing near active fires
- *(Presenter note: this slide will need to be updated each season with current contract information)*

**Slide 16 — GACC Structure and Dispatch**
- Geographic Area Coordination Centers — how the national dispatch system works
- NIFC coordination, how you get tasked
- The dispatch chain from national to local

**Slide 17 — Agency Relationships**
- USFS, BLM, CAL FIRE — who you may be working for
- You're operating under their contracts — understand the relationship
- Agency personnel are your partners, not your customers

**Slide 18 — Regulatory Framework**
- All flights under USFS agreements operate under FAR Part 135
- Squawk 1255 for firefighting operations
- TFRs: when it's busy, be aware — dispatch to one incident does not clear you through another incident's TFR
- NWCG PMS 505 — the US standard for aerial supervision

### Section 5: The Air Attack Role — Reinforcement (4 slides)

**Slide 19 — Your Job**
- Fly the aircraft, provide a stable observation platform for the AAS
- Maintain separation from terrain and traffic
- Stay ahead of the mission — anticipate AAS needs, manage fuel and endurance
- Systems, navigation, comms management

**Slide 20 — What You Don't Do**
- No transmissions on tactical frequencies — except safety of flight
- The AAS handles all tactical radio, target descriptions, airtanker coordination
- You monitor all frequencies and coordinate with AAS via intercom only
- Exception: if AAS is task-saturated and a critical call comes in, you can answer and brief AAS on intercom

**Slide 21 — CRM in the Air Attack**
- Monitor all frequencies, provide traffic callouts, assist during high workload
- Communicate proactively: fuel state, weather changes, performance limitations, system anomalies
- Withholding information erodes CRM — if something is developing, say it
- Both crew members back each other up

**Slide 22 — Key Tactical Reminders**
- 1,000 ft vertical separation from dissimilar aircraft
- Orbit at or above 1,500 ft AGL — hard floor
- 170 KIAS or less within FTA, 150 KIAS or less maneuvering
- Overhead pattern entry preferred
- FTA entry: communicate, clearance, comply — no entry without clearance

**Slide 23 — Audio Panel Management**
- You are monitoring multiple frequencies — use individual volume controls to manage them
- Highest priority (air-to-air) at highest volume
- Lowest priority (dispatch) turned down — but never turned off
- You are the backup for the ATGS. Your role in managing the airspace and maintaining situational awareness depends on hearing what's going on.
- Never turn a frequency off.

### Section 6: Safety & Bright Lines (4 slides)

**Slide 24 — Know Your Limits**
- Operate within your own envelope — not the airplane's, yours
- If you're out of touch with your own ability, you don't know where the edge is. And that's how people get killed in this business.
- Humility is not weakness. The pilot who thinks they're beyond learning is the one who puts themselves in a situation they can't handle.
- Aerial firefighting is high-risk. The mindset of honest self-assessment starts here. Now.
- Be the pilot who knows what they don't know — and keeps working to close the gap

**Slide 25 — Safety Culture**
- Safety is never compromised for the mission
- Coulson backs you when you make the safe call — no penalty, no questions
- Proactive risk mitigation, not reactive
- Report through SMS — that's how we learn and improve

**Slide 26 — Bright Lines**
- We've hired good people and we trust you. But there are lines that can't be crossed:
  - Integrity — honesty matters. If something happened, tell us. Lying about it is worse than the thing itself.
  - Willful disregard for standard operating procedures
  - Representing Coulson in a way that damages the company's reputation
  - Conduct that undermines trust — with your crew, with agency personnel, with the team
- This isn't about mistakes. Everyone makes mistakes and we'll work through them. This is about character.

**Slide 27 — FRAT and Tactical Pause**
- FRAT scoring: Green (go), Yellow (mitigation required), Red (not authorized)
- Tactical pause: either crew member can call it, at any time, no penalty
- If something doesn't feel right, say so. We'd rather have a conversation than an incident.

### Section 7: What You're Being Checked On (2 slides)

**Slide 28 — Training Form (TRN-04)**
- Ground school + flight training competency areas
- What you'll be signed off on before your OPC
- Covers: regulatory knowledge, mission planning, fire ops, aircraft handling, single-pilot operations

**Slide 29 — OPC Checkride (OPC-04)**
- ~1 hour evaluation flight, check pilot acts as AAS
- 26 evaluated items across 6 competency areas
- Automatic failure gates: safety event, repeated deficiency, checklist discipline
- Outcomes: Approved, Not Approved
- Annual recurrency required

### Section 8: Close (1 slide)

**Slide 30 — Chief Pilot's Message**
- You're part of something special at Coulson
- We're investing in you — take this seriously
- Use the air attack seat for what it is: the best seat in the house to learn aerial firefighting
- Perform well, learn constantly, represent Coulson with pride
- The tanker seat is earned, not given. This is where you earn it.
- Questions — open floor
